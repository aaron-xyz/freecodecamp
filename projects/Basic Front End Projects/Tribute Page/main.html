Richard Feynman

Richard Phillips Feynman

Richard Feynman was one of the most extraordinary scientist of the 20th century. As a brilliant physicist, he pioneered an entirely new area of his subject. He discovered a new law of nature, the most numerically precise physical theory ever invented. Yet, he scorned the Nobel Prize he received for his work.

Feynman's brilliance helped shape history. As a young man, he helped to build the atom bomb. And yet throughout his life, Feynman rejected authority and refuse to conform, preferring instead to follow his passions, from bongo playing to biology, from poetry to painting, from computing to cracking safes, Feynman fascinations with the world knew no bounds.

In his dying days as a maverick investigator on the Challenger shuttle disaster, he confronted the Washington stablishment to reveal the truth about what went wrong. Above all, Feynman infectius enthusiasm for life captivated millions of readers and viewers. One of the most captivating communicator in the history of science.

1918

He was born in 1918 in a small town called Far Rockaway, right on the outskirts of New York, near the sea. He lived there until 1935, He was seventeen. Then he moved to study at the MIT.

1939

He graduates from MIT after four years and then went to Princeton in about 1939.

1942

In 1942 he receive his PhD from Princeton and he got married with Arline, his first wife.

During that time he was at Princeton and started to work in the Manhattan Project, he ultimately went to Los Alamos in April in 1943 working with historical scientist like Niels Bohr and Robert Oppenheimer.

1946

He was at Los Alamos until something like October or November 1946, when he went to Cornell. Arline died of tuberculosis during that year.

1950

He was at Cornell until 1950. During that time he spent a sabbatical year teaching in Brazil.

1951

Feynman moves to CalTech like a theoretical physicist.

1963

Feynman delivers freshman lectures on physics at CalTech that become The Feynman Lectures on Physics. Delivers John Danz Lectures leading to The Meaning of It All.

1964

Delivers Messenger Lectures at Cornell University, which become the text for The Character of Physical Law. Elected foreign member of the Royal Society.

1965

Wins the nobel prize for the contributions to the understanding of the quantum mechanics in a new field, the Quantum Electrodynamics (QED).

1979

Delivers Sir Douglas Robb lectures on Quantum Electrodynamics at Auckland University, New Zealand, which become QED: The Strange Theory of Light and Matter.

1985

Serves as a member of the Presidential Commission investigating the Challenger space shuttle accident.
